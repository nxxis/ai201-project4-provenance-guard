## 1. Detection Signals

### Signal 1: Groq LLM Classification

**What it measures:**
Semantic and stylistic coherence holistically. The model is prompted to assess whether a piece of text reads as human- or AI-generated based on patterns such as hedged phrasing ("it is important to note that"), overly structured argumentation, uniform paragraph rhythm, and generic vocabulary choices.

**Output format:**
A float between 0.0 and 1.0, where 1.0 = high confidence AI-generated, 0.0 = high confidence human-written. The Groq prompt will instruct the model to return only a JSON object with a single `llm_score` field.

**Blind spots:**
A polished, deliberate human writer (academic, legal, technical) will produce text that reads as AI-generated to this signal. Conversely, a well-prompted LLM mimicking casual voice can evade it. This signal operates on perceived style, not ground-truth authorship.

---

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

**What it measures:**
Statistical structural properties of the text that differ between human and AI writing. AI text tends toward uniformity вҖ” consistent sentence lengths, moderate vocabulary reuse. Human writing is statistically messier.

**Specific metrics computed:**

1. **Sentence length variance** вҖ” the variance in word count across all sentences. AI writing clusters tightly around a mean; human writing is more irregular.
2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** вҖ” unique words divided by total words. AI writing at typical lengths shows moderate TTR; genuine human writing (especially casual or creative) is more lexically diverse.

**Combination into a single stylometric score:**
Both metrics are normalized to [0, 1] and averaged:

- Low sentence variance вҶ’ higher AI likelihood вҶ’ normalized score closer to 1.0
- Low TTR вҶ’ higher AI likelihood вҶ’ normalized score closer to 1.0

`stylometric_score = (normalized_variance_score + normalized_ttr_score) / 2`

**Output format:**
A float between 0.0 and 1.0, where 1.0 = highly uniform / AI-like structure.

**Blind spots:**
Non-native English speakers write more uniformly by necessity вҖ” limited vocabulary and shorter, simpler sentences score as "AI-like" under these metrics. Academic and legal writing is structurally regular for legitimate reasons. Haiku and minimalist poetry will score as AI-generated regardless of origin.

---

### Signal Combination

Both signals are combined into a single `confidence` score using a weighted average:

```
confidence = (0.6 * llm_score) + (0.4 * stylometric_score)
```

The LLM signal carries more weight (0.6) because it captures semantic patterns that pure structural metrics miss. The stylometric signal (0.4) acts as an independent structural corroboration вҖ” if the two signals strongly disagree, the combined score will land in the uncertain range, which is the correct behavior.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

### What confidence scores mean

| Score range | Interpretation                                          |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0.0 вҖ“ 0.35  | Likely human-written                                    |
| 0.36 вҖ“ 0.64 | Uncertain вҖ” insufficient signal to classify confidently |
| 0.65 вҖ“ 1.0  | Likely AI-generated                                     |

**What 0.6 means:** The system has weak evidence pointing toward AI-generated but cannot confidently assert it. Both signals would need to be moderately elevated (e.g., llm_score ~0.65, stylometric_score ~0.52) to land here. The label should communicate uncertainty, not a verdict.

**Why this threshold layout:** The uncertain band is intentionally wide (nearly 30 points) to reflect the spec's guidance that false positives (labeling a human's work as AI) are worse than false negatives. A score must reach 0.65 before the system asserts "likely AI." Below 0.35, it asserts "likely human." Everything in between gets an honest "we're not sure."

**Calibration approach:** The thresholds were set by running the four test inputs from the spec (clearly AI, clearly human, two borderline) and verifying each lands in the expected zone. If a clearly AI input scores below 0.65 or a clearly human input scores above 0.35, the signal weights will be adjusted before finalizing.

## 3. Transparency Label Design

Three label variants, keyed by confidence score range. These are the exact strings the API will return in the `label` field and that would be displayed to a reader on the platform.

### High-confidence AI (confidence вүҘ 0.65)

```
вҡ пёҸ AI-Generated Content Detected
Our analysis found strong indicators that this content was likely generated by an AI writing tool rather than written by the credited author. This label is based on automated detection and may not be perfectly accurate.
If you are the creator and believe this is incorrect, you can submit an appeal below.
```

### Uncertain (0.35 < confidence < 0.65)

```
рҹ”Қ Origin Uncertain
Our system could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. This may reflect a genuinely ambiguous writing style, mixed authorship, or the limits of automated detection.
If you are the creator and want to clarify the origin of your work, you can submit an appeal below.
```

### High-confidence human (confidence вүӨ 0.35)

```
вң… Appears Human-Written
Our analysis found no strong indicators of AI-generated content. This label reflects automated detection only and is not a guarantee of human authorship.
```

**Design rationale:** The asymmetry is intentional вҖ” the AI label includes an explicit appeal prompt because that is the high-stakes false-positive case. The human label is quieter; there is less urgency to appeal a favorable result.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

**Who can appeal:** Any creator who submits a `content_id` (returned from `POST /submit`) and a written `creator_reasoning` explaining why they believe the classification is incorrect.

**What they provide:**

- `content_id` вҖ” identifies the original submission in the audit log
- `creator_reasoning` вҖ” free-text explanation (e.g., "I am a non-native speaker and my writing is more formal than typical")

**What the system does on receipt:**

1. Looks up the original audit log entry by `content_id`. If not found, returns a 404 error.
2. Updates the entry's `status` field from `"classified"` to `"under_review"`.
3. Appends `appeal_reasoning` and `appeal_timestamp` to the log entry.
4. Returns a confirmation response: `{"confirmed": true, "status": "under_review", "content_id": "..."}`.

No automated re-classification is performed. The updated log entry is what a human reviewer would inspect.

**What a human reviewer sees in the log:**
The full original entry вҖ” `llm_score`, `stylometric_score`, `confidence`, `attribution`, `label` вҖ” plus the creator's `appeal_reasoning` and `appeal_timestamp`. This gives a reviewer everything needed to make a manual determination.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge case 1: Non-native English speaker writing formal prose

A creator whose first language is not English tends to write shorter, simpler sentences with a limited vocabulary range. The stylometric signal (low TTR, low sentence length variance) will score this as "AI-like" regardless of authorship. If the Groq signal also reads the formal register as AI-generated, the combined score could push into the uncertain or even AI zone. The wide uncertain band mitigates this вҖ” but a writer with confidence ~0.55 may still feel unfairly labeled. The appeal path is the primary mitigation here.

### Edge case 2: Minimalist or structured poetry

Haiku, couplets, and other constrained forms have extremely short sentences (low variance) and often repeat key words intentionally (low TTR). The stylometric signal will almost certainly misclassify these as AI-generated. The Groq signal may partially compensate if the semantic content reads as distinctly human, but a truly minimal poem (three lines, fifteen words) gives the LLM little to assess. Confidence scores for poetry in constrained forms will be unreliable.

### Edge case 3: Lightly edited AI output

A creator who generates a draft with an AI tool and then edits it substantially presents a genuinely ambiguous case. Neither signal was designed to detect mixed authorship вҖ” only endpoint classification (human vs. AI). The system will produce a score, but any result here is uncertain by definition. The label should reflect that, and this is exactly the scenario the uncertain band is designed to catch.

### Flow narrative

**Submission flow:** A POST /submit request carries `text` and `creator_id`. The text is passed independently to Signal 1 (Groq LLM, returns `llm_score`) and Signal 2 (stylometrics, returns `stylometric_score`). Both scores feed the confidence scorer, which applies a weighted average to produce a single `confidence` value. The label generator maps that value to one of three label variants. The full result вҖ” plus both individual signal scores вҖ” is written as a structured JSON entry to the audit log. The API returns `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, and `label`.

**Appeal flow:** A POST /appeal request carries `content_id` and `creator_reasoning`. The system looks up the original entry in the audit log by `content_id`, updates its `status` to `under_review`, and appends the appeal fields. No re-classification occurs. The response confirms receipt and current status.
